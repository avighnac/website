FAQ

This blog contains some questions that I've been asked multiple times by different people. I was inspired to make such a blog post by my friend, Samik's blog post. So, here it is!

Informatics

Q: How do I start / where do I begin?

A: When I was a beginner, I had the same question. It is true that, in a field as vast as competitive programming, knowing whether what you are doing is productive and helpful is a challenge in itself. While there is no mold that fits everyone, I personally think doing CSES extensively first and then attempting easier olympiads like the Singapore NOI—Preliminary Round is a great way to get started. Note that I do not believe that doing codeforces significantly helps with OI, but there is some value in reaching expert (1600), after which it becomes unoptimal.

Q: Can you share a roadmap or study plan?

A: A roadmap is hard to define. For more general tips, please refer to the previous answer. As for a study plan, I would recommend spending 5-6 hours, but this vastly varies amongst people. Some people prefer studying for longer, while some prefer studying for less time. You will have to experiment to find the right amount of time for you. However, my opinion is that, in order to improve at a decent rate, you need to do OI very actively and with determination, whatever that means for you. This is maybe similar to how you would think of your job in the future. Also, I'm working on an exciting new resource (no spoilers yet!) that plans to address this question, and will be released during SPOI 2027 🙂.

Q: How many hours a day did you practice?

A: This varied a lot with time. In 10th grade, when I first learned about OI, I practiced for around 5-6 hours every day, for around 2 months, because I was extremely motivated and didn't really see OI as 'studying'. After that, though, I had to focus on my 10th board examinations, and so I had to dial it down (to 0 hours). In 11th grade, I hardly did OI and probably averaged like 1.2 hours per day. In 12th grade, I was inconsistent at first, but after March (when my board exams got over), I probably averaged around 8-10 hours every day, for 3 months before IOITC.

Q: What's your Codeforces rating?

A: 1600. As a side note, I have noticed that asking this question is a sign that the asker will never do anything productive in life.

Q: C++ or Python — which should I use?

A: Definitely C++. It is not allowed to use Python in INOI, IOITC, and IOI (and APIO too). Also, most online resources will use C++.

Q: How do I get a gold in INOI?

A: I believe that, after a certain baseline, the amount of things one can achieve is virtually identical for almost everyone. The only separating factor is the amount of productive work you put in (not necessarily raw hours). While INOI gold is extremely competitive, and I only see it getting more competitive in future years, if you keep consistently improving and following your own study plan, it is achievable. I would also recommend consistently doing mock contests (not necessarily past INOI contests by the way), as this is something most beginners overlook the importance of.

Q: How do I do a mock contest?

A: Pick any olympiad that is close to the difficulty of the olympiad you are preparing for, and either set a timer, or use OI checklist's virtual contest feature, close discord, while sitting in an isolated room, and solve the problems. It is very important to treat this like the real exam.

Q: Can you look at my code and tell me why it's wrong?

A: I don't know how true this is for others, but, personally, I find it very hard to read code written by others (something I'm going to definitely have to work on!). As a result, I usually can't look at code and find your mistakes. I will be able to find your mistake way faster if you explain your logic to me.

Q: Is it too late for me to start?

A: That depends on what you want to achieve, and why you're doing this. If you're doing this for fun, the answer is, of course, no. If you're doing this for colleges, then it is not technically too late if you're below 12th grade. If you want to use OI as a backup option, it would be best if you were below 11th grade. However, this is a subjective question, and this is just my opinion.

Q: Do medals actually matter?

A: Yes.

A: So, I didn't, but this doesn't mean you won't have to. I didn't have to prepare for these exams because my knowledge from studying OI was sufficient to carry me through the more 'OI oriented' parts of the paper, of which there were surprisingly a lot. Q: Is SPOI free?

A: SPOI is free. However, we occasionally offer paid offerings, for example, the ZCO course. We offered financial aid for those who demonstrated need, and none of the major things in SPOI are paid.

Q: Will you personally review my progress?

A: If I see you putting in a lot of hard work yourself, then maybe I'll be interested 🙂. However, it is generally not a productive endeavour as I have noticed myself spending more effort getting people to put in effort than they are putting in themselves. I think it's obvious why this is an issue.

Academics and testing

Q: What was your SAT score, and how do I get one like it?

A: I got a 1500 on the SAT. Honestly, I didn't take the SATs as seriously as I should've, and I think a higher score is easily achievable with minimal prep time. I would recommend reviewing hard SAT questions and understanding why the answer... is the answer. For example, the English section looks extremely intimidating at first, but it can be trained for, and it does not test your general knowledge of English, only a very specific SAT relevant subset.

Q: How do I get a 5 on AP Calc BC / CS A?

A: For AP Calculus BC, I exclusively used Khan Academy with no books whatsoever and it was amazing. I recommend you do the same. The Khan Academy course is long though, it'll take you 2-3 months to get through it, but it is very fun. Getting a 5 on CS A is very easy, as it is just basic Java. I did not study for this exam.
